## Step 1: Choose Your State

### North Carolina (RECOMMENDED)

**Why NC wins for HopTrack:**

- **You live here.** No foreign LLC registration needed. That saves $250/yr and a second set of compliance paperwork.
- **Cheapest ongoing costs.** $200/yr annual report. That's it. No franchise tax, no separate business license fee.
- **Simple compliance.** One filing per year (April 15). One state to worry about.
- **You're a SaaS company, not raising VC.** Delaware's advantages (Court of Chancery, corporate law precedent) matter when you have investors demanding Delaware C-Corp structure. We're an LLC selling software to breweries. NC is fine.
- **Formation is fast.** Online filing, approved in 2-5 business days.

**NC formation cost:** $125 one-time + $200/yr annual report = **$325 year one**

### Delaware (SKIP IT)

The "default" choice for startups, but here's the reality:

- **Pros:** Business-friendly courts, well-established LLC law, no state income tax on out-of-state revenue, privacy (no member disclosure).
- **Cons for us:** You still need to register as a foreign LLC in NC ($250), pay DE franchise tax ($300/yr), DE business license ($75/yr), AND NC annual report ($200/yr). You're paying two states. The franchise tax alone is more than NC's entire annual cost.
- **When Delaware makes sense:** When you're raising institutional VC money, when you're a C-Corp, or when you have complex multi-state operations. Not us. Not yet.

**DE total cost if you live in NC:** $110 formation + $250 NC foreign reg + $300/yr DE franchise tax + $75/yr DE biz license + $200/yr NC annual report + $125/yr registered agent = **$760 year one, $900+/yr ongoing**

### Wyoming (INTERESTING BUT UNNECESSARY)

- **Pros:** Cheapest ongoing costs ($60/yr), no state income tax, strong asset protection, strong privacy.
- **Cons for us:** Same problem as Delaware — you'd need to foreign-register in NC ($250). Privacy and asset protection are nice but not critical for a SaaS startup. Added complexity for minimal benefit.

**WY total cost if you live in NC:** $100 formation + $250 NC foreign reg + $60/yr WY + $200/yr NC + registered agent = **$660 year one, $460+/yr ongoing**

### The Verdict

Form in NC. Save the money. If we raise a Series A and investors demand Delaware, we can convert later. Cross that bridge when we're counting our money.

## Step 2: Form Your LLC in North Carolina

### How to File (Online — Do This One)

1. Go to the NC Secretary of State website: **sosnc.gov**
2. Navigate to Business Registration > Online Filing
3. File **Articles of Organization** for a Domestic LLC
4. You'll need:
   - **LLC name:** "HopTrack LLC" (or "HopTrack, LLC" — check availability first at sosnc.gov)
   - **Registered agent:** You can be your own (free) — use your home address. Or use a service ($100-300/yr) for privacy.
   - **Principal office address:** Your address
   - **Organizer name and address:** Your name and address
   - **Effective date:** File date (immediate) or future date
5. Pay $125 online
6. Approved in 2-5 business days. You'll get a stamped copy back.

### Do You Need a Registered Agent Service?

**If you use your home address:** Your name and address become public record. Anyone can look it up. For a SaaS company, this is usually fine.

**If you want privacy:** Use a registered agent service ($100-300/yr). They provide a business address and receive legal documents on your behalf. Northwest Registered Agent and ZenBusiness are solid options.

**My take:** Save the money for now. Use your home address. Upgrade to a registered agent service when we have revenue.

## Step 3: Get Your EIN (Free, 10 Minutes)

An EIN (Employer Identification Number) is your business's Social Security number. You need it for everything — bank accounts, Stripe, taxes.

1. Go to **irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online**
2. Apply as: LLC
3. Member count: 1 (single-member LLC)
4. Reason: Started a new business
5. You get your EIN **immediately** — save/print the confirmation letter

**Important:** Do this AFTER your LLC is approved. The IRS needs your legal entity name.

**Cost:** Free. Always free. If anyone charges you for an EIN, they're scamming you.

## Step 4: Write Your Operating Agreement

Not legally required in NC, but **absolutely necessary**. This is your LLC's internal rulebook.

For a single-member LLC, it's simple. It documents:

- You are the sole member and manager
- How profits and losses are allocated (100% to you)
- Your capital contributions
- What happens if you add members later
- Dissolution procedures

**You can write this yourself.** There are free templates online (Northwest, LegalZoom, ZenBusiness all have them). Or pay a lawyer $200-500 to draft one.

**Why bother?** Banks want to see it. It proves you're operating as a real business entity. It protects your personal asset separation (the whole point of an LLC).

## Step 8: Taxes (The Part Nobody Likes)

### How LLC Taxes Work (Pass-Through)

As a single-member LLC, the IRS treats your business as a "disregarded entity." This means:

- Business income flows through to your personal tax return
- You file **Schedule C** (Profit or Loss from Business) with your personal 1040
- You pay **income tax** at your personal rate on net profit
- You pay **self-employment tax** (15.3%) on net profit — this covers Social Security (12.4%) and Medicare (2.9%)
- You can deduct half of the self-employment tax from your income

### What to Set Aside

**Rule of thumb: Set aside 30% of net profit for taxes.** This covers federal income tax + self-employment tax + NC state income tax. If you're in a higher bracket, bump that to 35-40%.

### Quarterly Estimated Taxes

Since nobody is withholding taxes from your LLC income, you need to pay estimated taxes quarterly:

- **Q1:** April 15
- **Q2:** June 15
- **Q3:** September 15
- **Q4:** January 15 (of the following year)

**Safe harbor rule:** Pay at least 100% of last year's tax liability (or 110% if your AGI exceeds $150K) to avoid underpayment penalties. First year? Estimate based on projected revenue.

Use **IRS Direct Pay** or **EFTPS** to make payments. Takes 5 minutes each quarter.

### The S-Corp Election (Future Optimization)

Once HopTrack is generating meaningful profit ($40K+ net), consider electing S-Corp taxation (IRS Form 2553). This lets you:

- Pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll tax)
- Take remaining profit as distributions (NOT subject to self-employment tax)
- Potentially save thousands in self-employment tax

**Example at $150K net profit:**
- As regular LLC: ~$21,200 in self-employment tax
- As S-Corp (paying yourself $70K salary): ~$10,710 in payroll tax
- **Savings: ~$10,500/year**

**When to do this:** When net profit consistently exceeds $40-50K. Requires payroll setup and additional tax filing (1120-S). Talk to a CPA when we get there.

### Deductions to Track From Day One

Start tracking these now — they reduce your taxable income:

- Hosting costs (Vercel, Supabase)
- Domain registration
- Software subscriptions (development tools, design tools)
- Stripe fees
- Home office (dedicated space, percentage of rent/mortgage + utilities)
- Internet (business percentage)
- Computer equipment
- Conference attendance / travel
- Marketing spend
- Contractor payments
- Professional services (accountant, lawyer)
- Business insurance premiums
- Education/training related to the business

Use a tool like **Relay** (built-in categorization) or **QuickBooks Self-Employed** ($15/mo) to track this automatically.

1. First brewery signs up and starts paying (get GL + cyber/E&O)

**Budget for insurance when we launch:** $120-180/mo for GL + Cyber/E&O bundle. Companies like **Vouch**, **Embroker**, and **Founder Shield** specialize in startup coverage and can quote you quickly.

## Step 10: SaaS-Specific Considerations

### Sales Tax (SaaS Nexus)

This is where it gets fun. SaaS sales tax rules vary by state:

- **Some states tax SaaS** as tangible personal property (looking at you, Texas, New York, Pennsylvania)
- **Some states don't** (California, most of the time)
- **NC:** SaaS is generally subject to sales tax in North Carolina

**What to do:** Once we have paying customers in multiple states, we'll need to track economic nexus thresholds. **Stripe Tax** can handle this automatically for an additional 0.5% per transaction. Worth it when we get there. Don't worry about it until we have paying customers.

### Collecting W-9s

If you pay any contractors over $600/year, you need to:
1. Collect a W-9 from them before paying
2. Issue a 1099-NEC by January 31 of the following year

### Business Licenses

NC doesn't require a general state business license for SaaS companies. Check your local city/county — some municipalities require a business privilege license (usually $25-100/yr). Asheville, for example, requires one.

## What NOT to Do

1. **Don't pay for an EIN.** It's free from the IRS. Period.
2. **Don't use LegalZoom/ZenBusiness for formation unless you want to.** NC's online filing is straightforward. Save the $200+ service fee.
3. **Don't incorporate in Delaware** just because TechCrunch said so. You're not raising a Series A next month. NC is perfect for now.
4. **Don't skip the operating agreement.** Even for a single-member LLC. Banks want it, and it protects you.
5. **Don't mix personal and business finances.** Separate bank account from day one. This is how you keep the LLC's liability protection.
6. **Don't forget quarterly estimated taxes.** The IRS will penalize you. Set calendar reminders.
7. **Don't overthink insurance pre-revenue.** Get it when the first brewery pays us. Not before.

## When to Get a CPA

You don't need one today, but you will. Here's when:

- **Revenue hits $50K/yr:** Time for a real accountant. They'll save you more than they cost.
- **Considering S-Corp election:** A CPA should run the numbers and handle the election.
- **First tax year with significant revenue:** Don't DIY your first complex return.
- **Budget:** $500-2,000/yr for a small business CPA. Worth every penny.
