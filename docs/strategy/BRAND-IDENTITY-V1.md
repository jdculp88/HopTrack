## 1. Brand Overview

### App Name Validation: Why "HopTrack" Works

**Verdict: Keep it. Full stop.**

The name does five jobs simultaneously, which is rare and worth protecting:

**1. Category signal without screaming it.** "Hops" is the ingredient that defines beer — more specifically, craft beer. Non-drinkers understand it. Enthusiasts respect it. It puts us in the right aisle without being as blunt as "BeerApp."

**2. The triple meaning of "Track."** Track your check-ins (the product). Track your journey (the narrative). Your track record with beer (the identity). Three resonant meanings for the price of one word. This is the kind of layered simplicity that brand strategists spend years and significant budget trying to achieve.

**3. It works as a verb.** "Did you HopTrack that?" / "I HopTracked Trillium last weekend." This is the Spotify/Google/Venmo test — the best app names become verbs. We pass.

**4. It's pun-adjacent without being try-hard.** There's a gentle wink in the name for those who catch it. Hops = beer. HopTrack = hop (jump) + track (follow). It's not punishing people with the pun, it's leaving it there for them to find. That's the right move.

**5. Practical searchability.** Clear spelling. Two syllables. No ambiguous characters. Works spoken aloud ("hop track") without confusion. Strong App Store keyword relevance for "beer tracker," "hop," and "craft beer."

**Names considered and rejected:**

| Name | What's wrong with it |
|------|---------------------|
| BrewLog | Sounds like a spreadsheet. No soul. |
| TapRoot | Strong, but reads as a tech company or a gardening app. |
| PintTrack | Too literal. Limits us stylistically. |
| CraftPath | Forgettable before you finish saying it. |
| Malty | Cute, but gives no indication of what the app does. |
| Cerveza | Clever but inaccessible. We're not a language app. |

### Brand Positioning Statement

**For craft drinkers who want more than a checklist, and breweries who want more than a listing — HopTrack is the beautifully designed, fiercely independent beer platform that turns every pour into a story worth sharing.**

Unlike other beer apps built in the early 2010s and left to age without care, HopTrack is designed for the people behind the bar and the people in front of it. We're not owned by a big brewery. We're not monetized through advertising. We're built from a genuine love of craft — and it shows in every pixel.

**One-sentence version (for elevator pitches and investor decks):**
HopTrack is the Spotify of craft beer — a beautifully designed platform where drinkers discover, track, and share their beer journey while giving independent breweries the digital tools they actually need.

### Brand Personality

Five adjectives. Each earns its place.

**1. Warm**
HopTrack feels like your favorite taproom on a Thursday evening. Familiar. Unhurried. The kind of place where the bartender remembers your order and your name. Warmth shows up in our color palette (amber, mahogany, cream), in our copy (we talk to people like people), and in our product decisions (we celebrate achievements, we don't shame you for checking in a domestic).

*Warm is not: saccharine, sentimental, or cloying. We're not a greeting card brand.*

**2. Knowledgeable**
We know our beer. We know the difference between a West Coast IPA and a hazy, between a Märzen and a Festbier. Our content, our achievement names, our feature copy — all of it signals genuine expertise. This gives us credibility with enthusiasts while still making room for beginners.

*Knowledgeable is not: gatekeeping, lecturing, or showing off. We never make anyone feel stupid for not knowing something.*

**3. Independent**
This is a values word, not just a marketing word. HopTrack is not owned by a major brewery conglomerate. We root for the little guys — the 20-barrel operation that's been open three years and has a line out the door on Fridays. Independence is in our DNA and it resonates deeply with craft drinkers who are acutely aware of who owns what in the beer world.

*Independent is not: contrarian, anti-establishment to the point of aggression, or smug about small-batch.*

**4. Playful**
We take craft beer seriously without taking ourselves seriously. There's room for jokes. There's room for puns (earned ones). Our achievement names, our empty states, our notifications — they can all land with a smile. The best brands in this space (think Duolingo, think Oatly, think BrewDog's early days) have a wit that makes people want to engage.

*Playful is not: juvenile, random, or edgy for the sake of edge. Every joke serves the brand.*

**5. Precise**
When we talk about data, stats, and insights — for both drinkers and breweries — we're specific and credible. "The #1 checked-in style in Denver this month is West Coast IPA" beats "people love hoppy beers." This precision, paired with warmth, is what makes us feel like a trustworthy friend who also happens to know a lot about beer.

*Precise is not: cold, clinical, or data-bro. Numbers are always in service of a human story.*

## 2. Visual Identity

### Color System Narrative

**Why warm dark + gold is the right choice for craft beer audiences — and why no competitor has figured this out.**

Every major beer app launched during or after the flat design era (2012–2018) defaulted to blue and white. They look like productivity tools. They feel like productivity tools. They are being used by people who'd rather be anywhere else.

We went the other direction, and it's deliberate.

**The psychology of warm dark:**
A warm dark palette — espresso blacks, mahogany surfaces, cream highlights — is what your brain registers when you walk into a great taproom after dark. It's candlelight. It's aged wood. It's the moment of anticipation before the first sip. Dark UI done wrong feels cold and technocratic. Warm dark done right feels like arriving somewhere special.

**The psychology of amber gold:**
Gold and amber are the colors of beer itself — lager, amber ale, golden ale, the foam catching the light. They're also historically associated with craftsmanship, quality, and earned achievement (trophies, medals, stamps). Our gold accent (`#D4A843`) hits that exact register: not gaudy, not timid. It rewards the eye when it appears.

**Why it works for our target audience:**
Our users are 25–42, urban, aesthetically literate. They buy specialty coffee. They own a few cookbooks they actually use. They notice when something is beautifully designed. The warm dark palette speaks to them the way a great label speaks to them in the bottle shop — before they've read a word.

**The named palette (use these names in briefs, not hex codes):**

| Name | Hex (Dark) | Hex (Light) | Role |
|------|-----------|------------|------|
| Espresso | `#0F0E0C` | — | App background (dark mode) |
| Mahogany | `#1C1A16` | — | Surface / card background (dark) |
| Parchment | — | `#FAF7F2` | App background (light mode) |
| White | — | `#FFFFFF` | Surface / card background (light) |
| Amber Gold | `#D4A843` | `#C49A35` | Primary accent, CTAs, highlights |
| Warm Amber | `#E8841A` | `#D4730E` | Secondary accent, warmth pops |
| Grain | `#8B7355` | `#7A6245` | Tertiary, subtle elements |
| Slate | `#6B7280` | `#9CA3AF` | Secondary text, metadata |
| Ash | `#9CA3AF` | `#6B7280` | Disabled states, placeholders |

**Color usage rules:**
- Amber Gold is the hero. Use it with intention, not generosity. One dominant gold element per screen.
- Never use pure white (`#FFFFFF`) as a background in dark mode. It breaks the warmth.
- In marketing materials, lead with dark. Light mode exists; it's not the identity.
- Avoid cool colors (blues, teals, purples) except for functional UI states (info, links).

### Typography Rationale

Three typefaces. Each has one job. They don't compete.

**Playfair Display — Editorial Authority**
A classic serif with high contrast and elegance. Playfair has roots in newspaper display type and editorial design — it signals that what you're reading matters. We use it for headlines, brewery names, and any moment where we want the reader to feel like they're encountering something worth their attention.

*Where it lives:* Headline type on the brewery detail page. "Year in Beer" reveal cards. Marketing headlines. App Store screenshots. Feature names.

*What it communicates:* This brewery has a story. This moment is worth commemorating. We're not a checklist app.

*Never use it for:* Body copy at small sizes (it becomes unreadable). Error states. Utility UI.

**DM Sans — Human Warmth**
A geometric sans-serif with soft terminals and excellent legibility across sizes. DM Sans doesn't call attention to itself — it just gets out of the way and lets the content breathe. Critically, it reads as warm rather than cold, which is not a given for geometric sans-serifs (see: Futura, which reads as authoritarian).

*Where it lives:* Body copy. Navigation labels. Button text. Notifications. Captions. Everything the app says in the course of normal operation.

*What it communicates:* We're easy to use. We're approachable. We're not going to make you work to read us.

*Never use it for:* Large editorial headlines (too quiet for that role). Stats and numbers (use Mono instead).

**JetBrains Mono — Technical Precision**
A monospaced typeface designed by JetBrains for developers — but it carries connotations that work perfectly for our data moments. Numbers rendered in Mono feel like they were measured, not estimated. Stats look like they mean something.

*Where it lives:* Check-in count displays. ABV and IBU readouts. Level indicators ("Level 4"). Achievement point totals. Brewery analytics figures.

*What it communicates:* This number is real. We know what we're talking about. The data is the data.

*Never use it for:* Body copy. Anything that needs to read as warm and human. Long strings of text.

**Type scale (working guidelines):**

| Level | Typeface | Weight | Use |
|-------|---------|--------|-----|
| Display | Playfair Display | 700 | Hero headlines, Year in Beer |
| H1 | Playfair Display | 600 | Page titles, brewery names |
| H2 | DM Sans | 600 | Section headers |
| H3 | DM Sans | 500 | Card titles, beer names |
| Body | DM Sans | 400 | All paragraph copy |
| Caption | DM Sans | 400 | Metadata, dates, secondary info |
| Stat | JetBrains Mono | 500 | Numbers, counts, levels |
| Label | DM Sans | 500 | Buttons, tags, badges |

### Logo Concept Descriptions

**Concept A — "The Hop Pin" (Primary Recommendation)**

A hop cone — the actual plant, not a cartoon — morphed organically into a location pin. The narrow pointed tip of a location pin is formed by the stem and base of the hop cone. The round body of the pin is the hop cone itself, rendered with enough detail to read as botanical but clean enough to work at 16px.

Set inside a filled dark circle (Espresso), with the hop pin in Amber Gold.

*Why it works:*
- Two meanings read instantly: beer (the hop) and location/tracking (the pin). No explanation needed.
- Works at every size: app icon, favicon, sticker, embroidery on a hat.
- The circle provides containment and shelf presence — it pops on any background.
- Avoids the pint glass silhouette that every brewery and beer brand already uses.
- The botanical accuracy of the hop cone signals craft knowledge without being snobby.

*Variations:*
- Full color: Amber Gold on Espresso circle
- Reversed: Espresso on Amber Gold circle (light backgrounds)
- Monochrome: White on dark (for merchandise, dark applications)
- Icon only (no wordmark): works at all sizes
- Icon + wordmark: horizontal lock-up for marketing materials

*This is the logo. Commission Concept A.*

---

**Concept B — "The Trail" (Marketing Illustrations)**

A stylized hop leaf — broader and more graphic than the Concept A cone — with a curved path or trail winding through it. The trail is the journey metaphor made literal: you start at one edge of the leaf and trace your way through, visiting breweries, discovering beers, building a history.

*Why it works:*
- More editorial and sophisticated than Concept A. Better for illustrated moments.
- Reinforces the "journey" narrative of the brand positioning.
- Can be used as a decorative element in Year in Beer cards, onboarding illustrations, and marketing headers.

*Why it's not the primary mark:*
- Too detailed for app icon and favicon. Falls apart below 48px.
- The path/trail concept doesn't read as quickly as the hop pin combo.

*Use it for:* Marketing illustrations, email headers, social content backgrounds, onboarding screens, printed materials.

---

**Concept C — "The Glass" (Retired)**

A pint glass silhouette with a hop cone as the foam head. Legible and immediately beer-coded.

*Why we're not pursuing it:*
- The pint glass silhouette is used by BrewDog, hundreds of craft breweries, multiple beer apps, and an uncountable number of taprooms. We'd be joining a crowd, not standing out.
- "Glass" imagery signals the consumption of beer, not the discovery and tracking of it. The narrative is off.
- No competitive differentiation whatsoever.

**Concept C is retired. Do not revisit without a compelling reason.**

---

### Icon / Favicon Recommendation

**App icon:** Concept A (The Hop Pin), full color (Amber Gold on Espresso circle), without wordmark. The circular container ensures it looks correct on both iOS (squircle) and Android (circle/squircle) icon treatments.

**Favicon:** Same mark at 32x32 and 16x16. At 16px, simplify the hop cone to the essential shape — the stem and three visible petals. The gold on dark reads clearly even at the smallest size.

**Social profile image:** The full icon (gold hop pin on dark circle) without wordmark. Square crop, no padding needed inside the circle.

**Marketing materials:** The icon paired with the wordmark "HopTrack" in Playfair Display, medium weight, in Amber Gold or Parchment depending on background.

## 3. Taglines

### Primary Tagline Recommendation

> **"Track Every Pour."**

**Why this is the right answer:**

Three words. Active verb. Inclusive noun. Does the job.

"Track" is our product verb — it's what the app does and it's in our name. "Every" is democratic — it doesn't matter if you're drinking your first craft IPA or your ten-thousandth check-in, this app is for every pour. "Pour" is more sensory and specific than "beer" or "drink" — it conjures the moment, the ritual, the anticipation.

It works at every size and context: app store subtitle, merchandise, billboard, Twitter bio, the loading screen. It doesn't try to be clever because it doesn't need to be.

It's already in the codebase as the app subtitle. It was right the first time. Keep it.

---

### 5 Alternative Taglines with Pros/Cons

**1. "Every Pint Has a Story."**

*What it's doing:* Emotional positioning. Frames the app as a storytelling platform, not a logging tool. Speaks to the narrative identity the brand is trying to build.

*Pros:* Warm, human, evocative. Works beautifully in lifestyle content — a photo of someone's first local sour ale with this caption is perfect. Strong for Year in Beer reveal cards.

*Cons:* Less functional than "Track Every Pour" — doesn't tell you what the app does. "Pint" may read as limiting (doesn't cover cans, bottles, flights). Can veer into the slightly precious.

*Best use:* Brand story moments, social content, Year in Beer card headers. Not the primary tagline.

---

**2. "Your Beer. Your Journey."**

*What it's doing:* Direct address ("your") makes it personal. "Journey" reinforces the progression and exploration narrative — you're building something over time, not just logging data.

*Pros:* Highly personal. Works well for onboarding copy. Pairs well with level-up moments ("Your journey. Level 8."). Strong for email subject lines ("Your Beer. Your 2026.").

*Cons:* "Journey" is mildly overused in app marketing. The two-sentence structure loses the punch of a single clear statement. Slightly passive — it describes your beer, doesn't invite you to act.

*Best use:* Onboarding screens, email campaigns, Year in Beer subject lines, profile page headers. Not the primary tagline.

---

**3. "Independent. Like Your Beer."**

*What it's doing:* Values-first positioning. Speaks directly to the craft community's awareness of and resistance to Big Beer ownership. Draws a straight line between the product's independence and the user's preference for independent breweries.

*Pros:* Hugely resonant with our core audience. Differentiates from RateBeer (owned by AB InBev) without naming names. Creates instant tribal recognition — craft drinkers know exactly what this means. Strong for ads, social, and earned media ("The beer app that's as independent as the beers you love").

*Cons:* Narrower appeal — it doesn't land as clearly with casual drinkers who don't think about brewery ownership. Can feel confrontational if overused. Not functional enough to carry the primary slot.

*Best use:* "About Us" page, trade show materials, podcast sponsorship reads, craft beer community outreach. Trot it out when speaking to the enthusiast segment.

---

**4. "The App That Knows Its Hops."**

*What it's doing:* Self-aware wordplay. "Know your hops" is a real phrase in beer culture (understanding hop varieties, flavor profiles, etc.). Applied to the app, it's a double meaning: we're a knowledgeable app AND we know what hops are.

*Pros:* Smart without being try-hard. Signals expertise with a wink. Good for social content where the audience appreciates clever. Could be a social bio in the right context.

*Cons:* Probably works better as a throwaway line in a tweet than as a brand tagline. "Know its hops" is ambiguous enough that non-enthusiasts might not catch what it's doing. Too inside-baseball for App Store or broad advertising.

*Best use:* Social media bios, community-facing content, fun moments in email copy. Never on paid ads or App Store listing.

---

**5. "Cheers to Keeping Score."**

*What it's doing:* Leans into the gamification angle. Makes tracking feel celebratory rather than data-obsessive. The "cheers" frame makes it feel like a toast, not a tally.

*Pros:* Works well for achievement moments and gamification-related marketing. The word "cheers" is universally understood as beer-related. Has a self-deprecating humor to it — yes, we know you're tracking this stuff obsessively, and we're toasting that.

*Cons:* "Keeping score" can feel slightly competitive or petty if over-literal. "Cheers to" is a common construction that might feel borrowed. Less versatile than the primary — doesn't scale to brewery-side messaging.

*Best use:* Achievement unlock moments, gamification feature marketing, social posts celebrating milestones. Not the primary tagline.

---

### Tagline Usage Guidelines

**Use "Track Every Pour" everywhere:**
- App Store name subtitle
- App Store screenshots
- Marketing site hero
- Social media bios (all platforms)
- Merchandise (hats, stickers, pint glasses)
- App loading/splash screen
- Email footer
- Investor decks (positioning slide)
- Press releases

**Use secondary taglines contextually:**
- "Every Pint Has a Story" — Year in Beer, lifestyle content, emotional moments
- "Your Beer. Your Journey." — onboarding, profile, personal milestone moments
- "Independent. Like Your Beer." — craft community outreach, brewery partner materials
- "The App That Knows Its Hops." — social media, community-facing content only
- "Cheers to Keeping Score." — achievement and gamification moments

**Never:**
- Use more than one tagline in the same layout. Pick one and commit.
- Modify the primary tagline ("Track Every Pour!" or "Track All Your Pours" — no).
- Use a secondary tagline in App Store copy unless it's been specifically approved for that context.
- Use taglines on brewery-side materials unless they've been adapted for B2B context.

### Feature Naming Conventions

Feature names should feel like they belong in the same world as the app. Not product-manager speak. Not marketing fluff. Names with personality.

**The Hop Club** — The digital loyalty stamp card system for breweries and consumers.

Why "The Hop Club"? Because "club" implies belonging, membership, and reward — exactly the feeling we want around loyalty. "The Hop Club" at your local brewery sounds like something worth being part of. Alternative considered: "Stamp Club" (too literal), "Brew Club" (generic).

*Usage:* "Join The Hop Club at [Brewery Name]" / "Your Hop Club card" / "Earn Hop Club stamps"

---

**Pint Rewind** — The weekly/monthly/yearly shareable recap feature.

Why "Pint Rewind" over "Year in Beer"? "Pint Rewind" is more ownable, more playful, and works for any time period (not just annual). "Year in Beer" is descriptive but generic — it tells you what it is rather than what it feels like. "Pint Rewind" has the same energy as "Spotify Wrapped" — it's a moment, an event, something you look forward to. It's also available as a brand name in a way "Year in Beer" is not.

*Usage:* "Your Pint Rewind is here." / "Weekly Pint Rewind" / "Your 2026 Pint Rewind"

Jamie prefers this over "Year in Beer" and recommends the team adopts it. Bring to Morgan for final call.

---

**The Board** — The TV display / video board for taprooms.

The team already calls it "The Board" informally. We should adopt this as the official product name. It's concrete, visual, and puts an image in your head immediately (the board behind the bar). Brewery owners will intuitively understand what "The Board" is without needing an explanation.

*Usage:* "Set up The Board in your taproom" / "Your customers' check-ins, live on The Board."

---

**HopTrack for Breweries** — The B2B product suite.

Keep it simple and clear. No clever names for the brewery-facing product. The audience (brewery owners) is busy and practical. They want to know what they're getting. "HopTrack for Breweries" is unambiguous.

*Brewery subscription tiers (already established by Taylor — confirmed correct):*
- **Free** — straightforward, no game-playing
- **Tap** — first paid tier, the tap is what you pour from
- **Barrel** — volume, commitment, the heart of the business
- **Cask** — premium, artisanal, the highest expression

This naming system is excellent. The escalation from Tap → Barrel → Cask maps to craft beer's own language of scale and prestige. Keep it.

---

**HopTrack Display** — The technical product name for `/display/[brewery_id]`. The Board is the consumer-facing name; "HopTrack Display" is used in documentation, settings, and the admin dashboard.

## 5. Target Audience Personas

### The Three-Part Audience

HopTrack serves three distinct consumer mindsets that often exist in the same person at different moments of their craft beer life. We design for all three — but we speak to them differently.

---

### Persona 1: The Craft Curious

**"I've been drinking IPAs from the grocery store. My friend just dragged me to an actual craft brewery and I want to know what I'm doing."**

**Age:** 24–32
**Location:** Urban or near-urban
**Background:** Grew up on domestic light lagers or avoided beer altogether. Recently started exploring craft — maybe through a food interest, a partner who drinks it, or a move to a city with a vibrant tap room scene.

**What they drink:** Session IPAs (approachable), wheat beers, fruit sours, whatever's on tap when they can't decide. Avoids anything that sounds too intense.

**Motivations:**
- Wants to feel competent and confident at a tap room, not like they're holding up the line
- Discovers that beer has real flavor complexity and wants to map it
- Loves the social element — beer as a shared experience with friends
- Responds well to visual beauty and design quality (this is what distinguishes them from the "just grab a Bud" crowd)

**Fears:**
- Being judged by bartenders or beer nerds for ordering something "wrong"
- Getting lost in jargon (IBU, DIPA, dry-hopped) and feeling stupid
- Spending $9 on a beer they hate

**What HopTrack gives them:**
- A safe space to learn. Our level system starts at "Hop Curious" — they are the level. That's intentional and welcoming.
- Language that teaches without lecturing. We explain what a hazy IPA is when they log one, but only if they want to know.
- The confidence to build a palate. They can look back at their check-ins and see themselves getting more specific over time.
- A reason to try the thing they wouldn't have ordered. Achievements and suggestions gently expand the comfort zone.
- The social proof of seeing what friends drink. "If Alex had that milk stout last night, maybe I could too."

**What we never do to them:**
- Make them feel embarrassed about what they're drinking (no dismissive copy about domestics)
- Overwhelm them with complexity on day one
- Gatekeep any part of the experience

### Persona 2: The Regular

**"I've been drinking craft beer for years. I have a rotating list of 8–10 favorites and I'm always adding to it. I'm not obsessive, I'm just into it."**

**Age:** 28–42
**Location:** Suburban to urban, has probably lived in or near a city with a strong craft scene
**Background:** Found craft beer in college or early career and never looked back. Has a home fridge with interesting stuff in it. Has opinions about hops.

**What they drink:** Has genuine variety. Follows a few local breweries closely. Takes brewery road trips. Has a "usual" at 2–3 places.

**Motivations:**
- Wants to remember what they drank (the memory function)
- Wants smarter recommendations — not algorithmic noise, but actually relevant suggestions based on what they've liked
- Genuinely enjoys seeing patterns in their own behavior ("apparently I drink 60% IPAs — huh")
- Values the loyalty angle: wants to feel recognized as a regular at the places they frequent
- Shares selectively — a great beer at a new spot is worth a post, but they're not going to caption every pint

**Fears:**
- An app that's more work than reward (logging fatigue)
- Being treated as a data point rather than a person
- Losing their check-in history if they switch apps (migration anxiety)

**What HopTrack gives them:**
- A beautiful, fast check-in experience that doesn't feel like homework
- Smart stats that surface insights they didn't know to look for ("Your favorite style changes by season")
- Pint Rewind (formerly Year in Beer) — a genuinely delightful artifact they didn't know they wanted until they saw it
- Loyalty integration at their regular breweries — stamps, rewards, recognition
- A social layer with enough activity to feel alive without turning into another feed to manage
- Level progression that reflects their real expertise without requiring them to perform it

### Persona 3: The Evangelist

**"My friends call me before they go to a new brewery. I've been on Untappd for 4 years and I'm bored. I want something that treats me like the craft drinker I actually am."**

**Age:** 27–45
**Location:** Could be anywhere — they'll drive 2 hours for a beer release
**Background:** Beer is a significant hobby and social identity. They've done brewery tours, attended GABF, joined a homebrew club or considered it, follow multiple brewery Instagram accounts. They are the person in their friend group who everyone defers to on beer.

**What they drink:** Everything, strategically. Actively hunts for new releases, rare finds, and beers from breweries people haven't heard of yet. Tracks ABV ranges, favorite breweries, style completion.

**Motivations:**
- Wants a platform that respects their depth of knowledge
- Wants to share recommendations and have them land with substance (not just a star rating)
- Wants community — a group of people who are as into this as they are
- Wants to be recognized for their expertise within the app
- Actively wants to support independent breweries — the RateBeer/AB InBev issue genuinely bothers them

**Fears:**
- An app that's dumbed down for casual users and doesn't have room for real depth
- Poor data integrity (bad beer listings, wrong brewery info, duplicate entries)
- A platform that's secretly aligned with Big Beer in any way
- Their social graph not existing yet on a new platform (starting over)

**What HopTrack gives them:**
- A platform that's genuinely, visibly independent — this matters to them more than almost anything
- The full level system with Grand Cicerone at the top — they want to work toward something that means something
- Pint Rewind as shareable content — this is the Year in Beer they'll actually post and their followers will actually engage with
- Social tools: friend activity, community check-ins, the taproom TV display where their name appears
- Achievement depth: the variety categories, the explorer paths, the streak systems — they'll find and pursue all of it
- Influence potential: early adopters in this persona are worth 10x a casual user in terms of word of mouth

**What we never do to them:**
- Condescend or slow them down with beginner friction
- Give them fewer stats than they want
- Pretend our database is perfect (be honest about gaps and let them help improve it)
- Ignore their feedback (they will have strong opinions and they're usually right)

## 6. Competitive Positioning

### The Context

The craft beer app market has a dominant incumbent built in 2010 with a 2012 design sensibility, owned by no one who cares about the craft community, and neglected in meaningful ways for most of the past decade. We are not here to take a swing at them. We are here to build something so genuinely better that the comparison makes itself.

All references in public-facing materials to this incumbent use the phrase "other apps" or "existing apps." We never name them. We don't need to.

---

### How We Talk About Ourselves vs. Other Apps

The principle: **we describe the world we're building, not the world we're leaving behind.** We never position ourselves as "the X alternative." We are HopTrack. What we are is enough.

**On design:**
- We say: "Built for the way you actually use beer apps — on a dimly lit bar stool or in a sun-lit taproom."
- We don't say: "Unlike other apps that haven't updated their design in 10 years..."

**On independence:**
- We say: "We're independently owned and operated. Nobody's telling us what to recommend."
- We don't say: "Unlike apps owned by major beer conglomerates..."

**On brewery tools:**
- We say: "Brewery tools built with as much care as the consumer app."
- We don't say: "Other apps charge breweries $300/month for tools that barely work."

**On data and privacy:**
- We say: "Your check-in data is yours. We use it to make your experience better, not to sell it."
- We don't say: "Other apps sell your data to distributors."

**The exception:** In direct sales conversations with brewery owners, Taylor can be more specific about competitive pricing and feature gaps. The "other apps" language applies to consumer-facing and public-facing communication.

---

### Messaging Hierarchy

**What we lead with:**

1. **Beauty.** The first thing anyone sees is the design. Let it do its job. "The most beautifully designed beer app ever made" is not a boast — it's an accurate claim and it's immediately verifiable by looking at it.

2. **Independence.** This is a values statement with a functional benefit: no one is paying us to recommend their beer. That matters to our audience and it's true.

3. **Both sides of the bar.** We uniquely serve both drinkers and breweries. This is the flywheel. Lead with it for brewery-facing audiences.

**What we save for later:**

4. **Specific features.** The TV display, the loyalty system, the analytics — these are powerful, but they're proof points that support the first three messages. Don't open with them.

5. **Stats and data.** Number of check-ins, number of beers in the database, brewery count — these matter once someone is already interested. They're not the hook.

6. **Comparisons.** If a brewery owner or journalist asks how we compare to the incumbent, we answer honestly and directly. We just don't volunteer the comparison unprompted.

---

### What We NEVER Say

These are the anti-patterns. They will kill the brand.

**"Crush it" / "smash it" / "kill it"** — Bro energy. Not us.

**"The ultimate [anything]"** — Superlatives without evidence are marketing noise. We say what we are, not what's ultimate.

**"Craft beer lovers"** — This phrase has been used by every beer brand marketing department since 2008. It means nothing anymore. Use "craft drinkers" or "people who care about what's in their glass."

**"We're not just an app, we're a community"** — Everyone says this. Show the community; don't claim it.

**"Best-in-class"** — This is something analysts say. We're not an analyst. We're a brand.

**"Seamless experience"** — Tech company speak. No one experiences anything seamlessly. They experience things that feel effortless. Say that.

**"At the end of the day"** — In any copy, anywhere.

**"Artisanal" applied to the app** — We can use it to describe beer. We never use it to describe our product. It would be insufferable.

**"Disrupting the [industry]"** — We're building something better. Disruption is what you call it when you don't have anything more interesting to say.

**"Your passion for beer"** — Passion is an overused word in marketing and it lands as slightly weird when applied to drinking. People don't have a passion for craft beer; they love it, they geek out on it, they chase it. Use those words instead.

**Anything that implies beer is primarily about getting drunk** — Our brand celebrates beer as a craft, a culture, and a community. Volume and intoxication are not the story we're telling.

## 8. Social Media Voice

### Instagram

**How we post:**
Instagram is our editorial home. Every post should feel like it was made by someone who drinks craft beer, lives in an interesting city, and knows how to take a good photo. The feed should be warm, cohesive, and visually consistent with the app — amber tones, real taproom settings, hands on glasses.

We post 4–5 times per week.

**What we post:**

| Content type | Frequency | Description |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| User check-in highlights | 2x/week | Reposted (with permission) check-ins from users at photogenic taprooms. Tag the brewery, tag the user. Builds community and provides social proof. |
| Brewery spotlights | 1x/week | A featured independent brewery. Short story in the caption. "This week's pour: [Brewery Name]." Drives brewery awareness and appeals to our independence positioning. |
| Achievement/Level moments | 1x/week | "Someone just hit Grand Cicerone. That's [X] check-ins, [X] breweries, [X] styles. Cheers." — Community celebration without over-engineering the post. |
| Pint Rewind excerpts | As they happen | When someone shares their Pint Rewind and it's beautiful, re-share it. This is organic marketing that works. |
| Brand copy/design cards | 1x/week | Short statement or tagline in brand design. "Every pint has a story." on a gold/dark card. These are shareable and brand-building. |

**Caption style:**

Keep it short. Instagram captions are not blog posts. The image carries the weight; the caption adds the voice.

*Example caption — Brewery spotlight:*
"Three years open. Line out the door every weekend. Still doing it because they love it.

Allagash Brewing, Portland, Maine. A HopTrack community favorite.

What's your go-to order?" [3–4 relevant hashtags]

*Example caption — Achievement moment:*
"Alex just became a Grand Cicerone. 500+ check-ins, 40+ breweries, and a palate that doesn't quit.

That's the energy.

Your journey starts at Hop Curious. Where are you?" [2–3 hashtags]

*Example caption — Design card:*
"Every pint has a story.

What's yours?" [1 hashtag: #HopTrack]

**Hashtag strategy:**
Use 3–5 targeted hashtags, never 30. Recommended: #HopTrack, #CraftBeer, #[CityName]Beer, #[BreweryName], #IndependentBeer. Rotate based on post content. Never use generic tags like #beer or #drink alone — too broad to be useful.

**What we don't post:**
- Stock photos of beer
- Anything that looks like an ad
- Aggressive CTAs ("Download NOW!")
- Anything mentioning competitor apps by name
- Alcohol content as the point (e.g., posts that are primarily about getting drunk)

**What we don't do on Twitter:**

- Subtweet competitors

- Use the platform for customer support (DMs or dedicated channel for that)

### TikTok

**The opportunity:** TikTok's craft beer community is genuinely large and underserved by quality content. Brewery tours, style explainers, and "what should I drink next?" content perform well. We can own a distinct corner of that space.

**Our lane:** Educational content that feels like it came from a friend, not a brand. The voice is warm, knowledgeable, and slightly self-aware. We never try to be trendy; we just try to be interesting.

**Content concepts that fit the brand:**

**"The First Time I Had a [Style]"** — UGC-style first-person storytelling about discovering a beer style. Works great as a trend/duet format. Drives identification and sharing.

*Format:* Talking head or screen recording with voiceover. 30–60 seconds. "The first time I had a West Coast IPA I thought something had gone wrong with it. Now it's 40% of my check-ins. HopTrack made me realize that."

---

**"Style in 60 Seconds"** — What is a hazy IPA? What is a Märzen? Fast, accessible explainers that don't talk down to people. The app's data makes us credible for this content.

*Format:* Fast cut, graphic text overlays, voiceover. Brand colors throughout. Ends with: "What style should we cover next?"

---

**"What the HopTrack Data Says"** — Use real aggregate app data (anonymized) for trend content. "The most checked-in beer style in [City] this month is..." — this is genuinely interesting and completely unique to us.

*Format:* Data reveal structure. Start with a question, hold, reveal. Could be a series.

---

**"Brewery Unlocks"** — Showcase a brewery that recently joined HopTrack or hit a check-in milestone. Short, celebratory, drives brewery awareness.

*Format:* Quick cut of taproom footage (user-generated or brand-captured). Ends with brewery's HopTrack page.

---

**"Pint Rewind Reveals"** — When Pint Rewind launches, seeded content of people reacting to their year-end recaps. The emotional reaction is the content.

*Format:* Screen recording of the Pint Rewind card animating in, with genuine reaction. This is the format that goes viral.

---

**What we don't do on TikTok:**
- Chase trends that have nothing to do with beer or the brand
- Use the platform to talk about promotions or pricing
- Post anything that feels like an ad
- Disparage other platforms or apps
- Create content that requires audio to make sense (always caption-design for mute viewing)
