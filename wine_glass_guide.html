The shape of your glass is not snobbery — it's science. Bowl width controls oxidation, rim diameter directs wine to different taste zones on your palate, and stem length keeps temperature in check. Every curve has a purpose.

Bordeaux Glass
Cabernet Glass · Tall Red Wine
Tall and broad with a slightly tapered rim. Designed for full-bodied reds with high tannin — the wide bowl lets the wine breathe and the height directs it to the back of the palate where bold flavors register best.

Burgundy Glass
Pinot Noir Glass · Balloon Red
The widest red wine bowl — almost balloon-shaped — with a narrower rim that funnels delicate aromatics. Captures the nuanced cherry, earth, and floral notes of lighter-bodied reds that would get lost in a Bordeaux glass.

White Wine Glass
Chardonnay Glass · Standard White
Smaller than its red counterparts to keep white wine colder longer. The U-shaped bowl allows aromas to develop while the narrower opening preserves floral and citrus top notes. Versatile enough for most whites.

Sauvignon Blanc Glass
Aromatic White Glass · Tall White
Taller and slimmer than a standard white wine glass. The narrow bowl captures and focuses bright, herbaceous aromas — cut grass, lime zest, gooseberry — and delivers the wine to the tip of your tongue for maximum acidity perception.

Champagne Flute
Sparkling Wine Flute
The narrow column preserves carbonation and sends a constant stream of fine bubbles upward, creating the visual spectacle that sparkling wine demands. Keeps the wine colder longer and delivers a focused, crisp sip.

Champagne Coupe
Saucer · Marie Antoinette Glass
The original champagne glass — wide, shallow, and romantically vintage. It lets bubbles escape quickly, which some sommeliers actually prefer for aged champagne. Iconic for cocktails and champagne towers alike.

Rosé Glass
Flared Lip White · Rosé Flute
A subtle flare at the lip is the key — it directs wine to the tip of the tongue where sweetness is perceived first, then lets acidity clean the finish. Designed to balance the fruit-forward, bright character of rosé.

Port Glass
Dessert Wine Glass · Fortified Glass
Intentionally small — dessert wines and fortified wines are served in smaller portions due to their intensity and sweetness. The narrow bowl concentrates rich plum, chocolate, and caramel aromatics while limiting the pour size.

Copita
Sherry Glass · Catavino
The official nosing glass of Jerez. This narrow tulip shape captures the volatile, complex aromatics of sherry — from saline fino to rich oloroso — and delivers them in concentrated waves. Also the standard for whisky nosing.

Universal Wine Glass
ISO Tasting Glass · All-Purpose
The do-everything glass. Slightly egg-shaped bowl, moderate size, and a gentle taper at the rim. It won't be optimal for any single varietal but handles everything competently — the smart choice for a home bar that doesn't want twelve different stems.

Stemless Wine Glass
Tumbler · Modern Wine Glass
The casual option. No stem means no breakage risk, dishwasher-friendly, and easy to stack. Purists will note that hand warmth affects temperature, but for everyday drinking and outdoor gatherings, it's the practical modern standard.
Best Served With
Everyday Reds

Riesling Glass
Aromatic Wine Glass · Rhine Glass
Tall and slender with a narrow opening to capture and intensify the perfumed aromatics Riesling is famous for — petrol, peach, honeysuckle. The slim shape keeps serving temperature cold and focuses each sip precisely.
