Five marks I'd put on a mug
You asked what I like. So here's what I like: marks that don't try to impress you. Marks that sit quietly on a surface and make you trust whatever they're next to. The kind of line that feels like someone drew it once, got it right, and stopped. No second pass. No cleanup. Just confidence.

These are the five I'd stamp into the bottom of a ceramic mug. That's the highest compliment I have.
MP-1
MP-1
The Quiet Spiral
One line. One spiral. Enters at the crown, traces the outer hop silhouette wide, then curves inward in a single open spiral that settles near the center. It doesn't close — it just arrives. The stem drops clean with one small leaf.
This is the one I keep coming back to. It's the mark that doesn't raise its voice. It doesn't need to. It's the one I'd stamp into every mug I make.

The Half Turn
Clean hop silhouette, closed. Inside: one single swoop that crosses from wall to wall, curls once, and stops. Not a full spiral. Not a knot. Just one confident interior gesture. Like someone started to explain and realized one stroke said everything.
This is the one that would work at every single size. Favicon, billboard, mug bottom, business card. It never gets confused. It never gets lost.

The River
No outline. No boundary. Just the line itself — flowing from top to bottom through three gentle bends that widen as they descend, naturally carving out a hop-cone shaped space. The silhouette exists only in the negative space the river creates. The most reduced mark possible. If you can remove nothing else, you're done.
This is the one I'd be nervous to show because it's almost too simple. But that's exactly why I trust it. It's the mark that has nothing to prove.

The Embrace
Two strokes start from the same point at the crown, sweep in opposite directions around the hop body, and meet again at the stem. The space between them is the hop. Two subtle interior swoops bridge the halves. It's a hop drawn by two hands moving in mirror — or one hand drawing a hug around empty space.
I know this technically breaks the single-line rule. I don't care. Some hops need two arms.

MP-5 · The one I'd ship
The One
BP-1's soul in BP-20's body. One line enters at the crown, traces the outer hop shape wide, then loops inward with one confident turn — not too tight, not too loose — before settling near the center. The stem drops clean. One line. One loop. One hop. Done.
This is the one. Not because it's the most clever or the most complex. Because it's the one where the line found exactly the right path and stopped looking. That's good design. That's good code. That's a good pour. Know when you've arrived.
